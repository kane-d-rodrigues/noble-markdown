What is FCOM 111?

Basically a civics course that teaches you about government and how to think about how we organise our society.

This course sets the context for the BCom degree, acquainting students with the legal and governmental environment that NZ firms operate in.

FCOM stands for 'Faculty of Commerce'.

Critical Thinking

What does "Critical Thinking" mean?

Things may not be as they seem.

We are concerned with how society really works.

We should subject our own thinking (and those of institutions) to regular evaluation.


Why is critical thinking important in academics?

In academic activity, we need to be wary of common sense.

We all carry biases.

The best remedy is "Critical Thinking".

Critical thinking involves thinking about what is fair.

In this course, this is useful for generating paragraphs in your essays.


You can apply a process of the following:

Isolating a claim

Looking for evidence

Applying reason (logical thinking)


Examples of sloppy thinking

  • 'I've never heard of that before, so it must not be true'
  • 'I don;t believe it, therefore it isn't true'
  • 'That's not my opinion, so it can't be right'


These are assumptions!

That is something we take for granted to be true or false without evidence.

We often overvalue what we see, and negativity sells content.

One example shown is through the allocation of stories towards crime and its error in representation in proportion to actual crime rates.


Why do we critically think?

  • Too much information
  • There is too much information in the world so we have to filter
  • Our brains use techniques to cope (these are called "heuristics")

  • Cognitive Limits
  • We don't necessarily see all that we need to make good decisions
  • Our brains connect the dots as best they can


Building Good Practices

One negative outcome from a lack of critical thinking is the use of stereotypes to assume ideas on an individual based on the group they come from.

This can be seen in a positive manner, through the halo effect.

This can also be seen in a negative manner, through the use of racism.


We overvalue what we want to believe

This can extend even to the point of ignoring contrary evidence (as seen in confirmation bias).


How do we build good practices?

  • Think critically
  • Gather evidence
  • Check for your own biases
  • Assess options
  • Then move to a view/decision
  • Stay open to new evidence / revising your view


Good practices involve referencing in all stages of a researching process

Good critical thinkers spend time researching to gather information and a range of views before drawing conclusions

They have a preference for original material, and for authoritative sources such as academic publications and government reports

With the use of good practices, readers are able to check sources and replicate the work


Writing

An essay is essentially a conversation with the reader

It starts with a claim or proposition

It backs up this claim or proposition with evidence

It is clear about the quality and weakness of the provided evidence

It draws a conclusion


An Example of An Essay

  • Claim - It might rain today
  • Evidence - The weather report is for rain
  • Evidence - Look at the black clouds above us
  • Conclusion - The claim is likely correct


Tips for Essay Writing:

When writing an essay, make sure that you can follow all steps of an essay writing process

Start with a claim or proposition

Back it up with Evidence (carefully selected and analysed)

Present a balanced assessment (fair and objective)

Arrive at a reasoned conclusion
